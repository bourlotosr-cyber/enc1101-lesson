Integrating Research Effectively

Using sources to build arguments — not just fill space

Why does this matter?

Research papers fail not because students can't find sources — they fail because sources are dropped in without explanation, paraphrased so closely they become plagiarism, or quoted so often the writer's own voice disappears. The CRE method (Claim · Evidence · Reasoning) gives you a repeatable structure that keeps your voice central and your reasoning visible.

Learning Objectives

1
Apply the CRE method — Claim, Evidence, Reasoning — to every body paragraph
2
Explain how evidence supports your claim through the Reasoning step
3
Avoid dropped quotations and patchwriting
4
Synthesize multiple sources into a unified analysis
5
Place sources in conversation with each other
6
Apply MLA 9 citation format accurately

Section 1 of 5
The CRE Method

A three-part structure that keeps your argument, evidence, and analysis working together

C Claim

Your debatable assertion — what you are arguing in this paragraph. Always your own words. Never open a paragraph with a quote.

R Evidence

A quote, paraphrase, or summary from a source, introduced with a signal phrase and cited in MLA 9.

E Reasoning

Your explanation of how and why the evidence proves your claim. This is the most important — and most commonly missing — step.

The Reasoning step is where most students lose points

Reasoning is not a restatement of the evidence — it is your analysis of what the evidence means for your specific argument. Ask yourself: So what? What should the reader conclude from this evidence?

Before and After: CRE in Practice

✖ Without Reasoning
Social media has a negative impact on teenagers. "Adolescents who spend more than three hours per day on social media platforms show significantly higher rates of anxiety and depression" (Twenge 47). This is a serious problem that needs to be addressed.
⚠ There is Evidence, but no Reasoning. "This is a serious problem" restates the obvious rather than connecting the evidence to the argument or explaining what the reader should conclude.
✓ With CRE: Claim → Evidence → Reasoning
Claim
Social media's design actively encourages the prolonged engagement that harms adolescent mental health.
Evidence
Psychologist Jean Twenge's longitudinal study found that teenagers who spend more than three hours daily on social platforms show significantly elevated rates of anxiety and depression (47).
Reasoning
This correlation matters because it reveals a dose-response relationship — the more time spent, the greater the harm — which suggests that platform usage is not incidental but causal in adolescent mental health decline. Regulation of design features that maximize screen time is therefore a public health issue, not merely a personal choice.
✓ The Claim sets up exactly what the paragraph will prove. The Evidence is introduced and cited. The Reasoning connects the evidence back to the claim and explains the "so what" — the implication that drives the argument forward.

Signal phrase + paraphrase + parenthetical:

Psychologist Jean Twenge's longitudinal study found that teenagers who spend more than three hours daily on social platforms show significantly elevated rates of anxiety and depression (47).

Works Cited entry:

Twenge, Jean M. iGen: Why Today's Super-Connected Kids Are Growing Up Less Rebellious, More Tolerant, Less Happy — and Completely Unprepared for Adulthood. Atria Books, 2017.

Quick Check ✓

Look at a body paragraph from your draft. Can you label each sentence C, R, or E? If a sentence doesn't fit any label — or if your Reasoning (E) is missing entirely — that's where your paragraph needs work.

Section 2 of 5
Dropped Quotations

A dropped quotation is Evidence with no Claim to set it up and no Reasoning to explain it

What is a dropped quotation?

A quotation that stands alone — either at the start of a paragraph, between unrelated sentences, or with only a citation and nothing else. In CRE terms: the two most important parts — Claim and Reasoning — are missing. The reader is left to guess what conclusion to draw.

Three Patterns to Avoid

1
Opening a paragraph with a quote — Your Claim, in your own voice, must always come first. Never let a source speak before you do.
2
Quote immediately followed by another quote — Evidence stacked on Evidence with no Reasoning between them. This is two dropped quotations, not one.
3
Closing a paragraph with a quote — The Reasoning step must be the last thing the reader sees. Never let Evidence have the final word.

Before and After

✖ Two dropped quotations, no Reasoning
Minimum wage affects workers differently depending on their industry. "Raising the minimum wage to $15 per hour would lift 900,000 Americans out of poverty while causing 1.4 million job losses" (CBO 3). "Low-wage workers are disproportionately employed in the food service and retail sectors" (Bureau of Labor Statistics 12). These findings are important.
⚠ The paragraph opens with a Claim, but then stacks two quotations with nothing in between. "These findings are important" is not Reasoning — it's a placeholder. No signal phrases introduce either source.
✓ CRE applied: both sources introduced and explained
Claim
The economic tradeoffs of a $15 federal minimum wage are genuinely contested, and the data must be read carefully rather than selectively.
Evidence
The Congressional Budget Office projects that such an increase would lift approximately 900,000 Americans out of poverty but simultaneously estimates 1.4 million job losses (3).
Reasoning
Understanding who bears those losses is essential: the Bureau of Labor Statistics reports that low-wage workers are concentrated heavily in food service and retail (12), meaning job cuts would fall hardest on the very population the wage increase is intended to help. This tension does not mean the policy is wrong, but it does mean a flat federal floor may not account for regional labor market variation.
✓ The Claim signals what the paragraph will argue. Both sources are woven into Evidence and Reasoning. The paragraph ends with the writer's own analytical conclusion — not a quotation.

Use a signal phrase before every piece of Evidence. Vary the verb to reflect what the source is doing:

  • According to economist David Autor, ...
  • The EPA's 2023 report concludes that ...
  • Sociologist Mary Pattillo argues that ...
  • As clinical psychologist Lisa Damour observes, ...
  • A 2022 Johns Hopkins study found that ...
  • The Congressional Budget Office projects that ...

Vary your signal verbs: argues, contends, observes, reports, demonstrates, suggests, asserts, notes, explains, warns, cautions, emphasizes.

Section 3 of 5
Patchwriting

Swapping synonyms is not the same as paraphrasing — and it shows you haven't done the Reasoning step

Patchwriting skips the Reasoning step

Patchwriting is replacing a few words from a source with synonyms while keeping the same sentence structure. It is a form of academic dishonesty even when you cite the source, because it demonstrates that you haven't processed the idea well enough to express it in your own words. A true paraphrase — one that supports genuine Reasoning — requires real comprehension.

Worked Example

Original source (Kendi, How to Be an Antiracist, p. 18):

"The opposite of racist isn't 'not racist.' It is 'antiracist.' What's the difference? One endorses either the idea of a racial hierarchy as a racist, or racial equality as an antiracist. One either believes problems are rooted in groups of people, as a racist, or locates the roots of problems in power and policies, as an antiracist."
✖ Patchwriting — synonyms swapped, structure unchanged
Kendi explains that the reverse of racist is not "not racist" but rather "antiracist." The distinction is that one either supports the notion of a racial hierarchy as a racist, or racial equivalence as an antiracist. One either thinks difficulties are caused by groups of people, as a racist, or finds the source of difficulties in authority and policies, as an antiracist (18).
⚠ Almost every phrase is lifted from the original with only synonym substitutions ("reverse" for "opposite," "equivalence" for "equality," "authority" for "power"). The sentence structure is identical. No Reasoning is added — the writer never explains what this distinction means for their argument. This is patchwriting even with a citation.
✓ CRE applied: true paraphrase + genuine Reasoning
Claim
Passive non-racism is insufficient — workplaces that train employees merely to avoid racist behavior are missing the deeper structural critique antiracism requires.
Evidence
Ibram X. Kendi draws a line between claiming not to hold racist views and actively being antiracist, which he defines as believing in genuine racial equality and attributing racial disparities to policy failures rather than to deficiencies within racial groups (18).
Reasoning
This distinction matters for the debate over implicit bias training: programs that teach employees to be "not racist" may produce comfort without accountability, while Kendi's framework demands policy-level thinking. An organization that focuses only on individual attitudes — rather than examining its own hiring, promotion, and pay structures — is, by this definition, doing nothing.
✓ The paraphrase uses the writer's own sentence structure. The Reasoning connects Kendi's definition to a specific policy debate — proof of genuine comprehension, not just rearrangement.
  • Cover the original. Can you rewrite the idea without looking at it?
  • Is your sentence structure different from the source's?
  • Did you use your own vocabulary, not just synonyms?
  • Does your Reasoning add interpretation — not just restatement?
  • Could a reader tell this is your analysis, not the source's words reshuffled?

If you answered "no" to any of these, revise before submitting.

Section 4 of 5
Synthesizing Multiple Sources

The Reasoning step is what transforms a list of sources into an argument

Synthesis means weaving multiple sources together under a single Claim and a unified Reasoning step, rather than summarizing them one at a time. Without Reasoning, a multi-source paragraph is just a list.

Source A one finding
+
Source B related finding
+
Source C complicating evidence
Your Reasoning: what do these sources together prove that none could prove alone?

Before and After

✖ The "tennis match" — sources summarized separately, no Reasoning
Gun violence in America is a serious issue. Everytown for Gun Safety reports that there are more than 45,000 gun deaths per year in the United States (Everytown 1). Researcher John Donohue argues that stricter gun laws reduce homicide rates (Donohue 214). However, economist John Lott argues the opposite (Lott 88).
⚠ The paragraph has a Claim and three pieces of Evidence, but Reasoning is completely absent. The paragraph bounces from source to source without telling the reader what to conclude. This is a literature list, not an argument.
✓ CRE applied: sources woven under a single Reasoning step
Claim
The relationship between gun legislation and mortality rates is empirically contested, but the weight of peer-reviewed evidence favors stricter regulation.
Evidence
While Everytown for Gun Safety documents more than 45,000 annual gun deaths (1), Stanford law professor John Donohue's analysis of state-level data over three decades finds consistent reductions in homicide rates following the adoption of permit-to-purchase laws (214). Economist John Lott's counterargument — that concealed carry laws reduce crime — has faced sustained methodological critique, including from the National Academy of Sciences, which found his data insufficient to support the conclusion (Wellford et al. 6).
Reasoning
Read together, the research suggests not that gun laws never work, but that the specific mechanism matters enormously. Donohue's findings hold under rigorous peer review while Lott's do not, which means the debate is not truly balanced — it only appears that way when credibility is treated as equal across sources. Policymakers who invoke Lott to resist regulation are citing a study the scientific community has already evaluated and found wanting.
✓ All three sources appear in the Evidence. The Reasoning evaluates them against each other and draws a conclusion — one that only becomes possible by reading the sources together.
  • While [Author A] finds X, [Author B]'s research complicates this by showing...
  • This finding aligns with [Author B], who similarly argues that...
  • Both [A] and [B] agree that X, though they differ on...
  • Read together, these studies suggest that...
  • [Author B]'s work extends [Author A]'s claim by demonstrating...
  • Unlike [Author A], who argues X, [Author B] contends that...
Section 5 of 5
Sources in Conversation

The most sophisticated form of the Reasoning step — showing that researchers respond to each other

Putting sources in conversation means your Reasoning step shows how researchers are responding to each other — agreeing, disagreeing, qualifying, or extending one another's arguments. It demonstrates you understand the academic debate, not just individual sources.

Source 1 (2018)
"Charter schools show no consistent advantage over traditional public schools in reading scores."
Source 2 (2021)
"Urban charter schools demonstrate significant gains in math achievement among low-income students."
↓ your Reasoning step puts these in conversation ↓
The contradiction dissolves when examined geographically: school type matters less than the socioeconomic context in which it operates, suggesting the debate should focus on resource equity rather than governance model.

Before and After

✖ Sources acknowledged but not in dialogue — Reasoning absent
Education researchers disagree about charter schools. CREDO's 2019 national study found mixed results for charter school performance (CREDO 4). However, Angrist et al. found that Boston charter schools produced large gains in math (Angrist 22). These studies show different things about charter schools.
⚠ The sources are acknowledged to disagree, but the Reasoning never explains why they disagree, mediates the tension, or draws a conclusion. "These studies show different things" is a restatement, not analysis.
✓ CRE applied: Reasoning mediates the scholarly disagreement
Claim
The debate over charter school effectiveness reveals how much study design shapes outcome — and why "charter school" is too broad a category for meaningful policy conclusions.
Evidence
CREDO's broad 2019 national analysis, aggregating data across 41 states, found inconsistent results — charter students performed better in math but showed no meaningful difference in reading (4). Angrist and colleagues' study of Boston-area charters found dramatically different results, with students gaining the equivalent of nearly a full year of additional learning in math annually (22).
Reasoning
These findings are not necessarily contradictory: CREDO's national sample includes a wide range of charter quality, while Angrist's sample captures a cluster of high-performing, "no-excuses" urban charters. What the research collectively suggests is that the question is not whether charters work, but which models, under what conditions, and for which students — a distinction that most policy debates, which treat charter schooling as a monolith, fail to make.
✓ The Reasoning explains the methodological reason the two sources appear to conflict, resolves the tension, and draws a conclusion more nuanced than either source alone could produce.

Angrist, Joshua, et al. "Explaining Charter School Effectiveness." American Economic Journal: Applied Economics, vol. 5, no. 4, 2013, pp. 1–27.

CREDO. Charter School Performance in 41 States. Center for Research on Education Outcomes, Stanford University, 2019.
